# ADVANCED MIXING TECHNIQUES & PSYCHOACOUSTICS

## FREQUENCY MAP

### Sub Bass (20-60Hz)
- Physical sensation, felt more than heard
- Kick drum fundamental (50-60Hz), sub bass root (40-60Hz)
- Keep MONO in this region — stereo sub causes phase problems and club system issues
- Too much: muddy, loses definition on small speakers
- Too little: thin, lacks power on club systems

### Bass (60-250Hz)
- 80-120Hz: body/weight of bass — boost for thickness
- 100-200Hz: muddiness zone — cut if mix sounds wooly or thick
- 200-250Hz: warmth — a little adds body, too much = mud

### Low Mids (250-500Hz)
- "The mud zone" — always suspect here first
- 300-400Hz: boxy sound on snare/vocal — cut if hollow
- 400-600Hz: nasal quality — cut if honky or nasal
- Almost always cut, rarely boost

### Midrange (500Hz-2kHz)
- Where most instruments live
- 1-2kHz: bite and attack — boost for aggression and presence
- 1.5kHz: harshness zone — cut if mix sounds aggressive/harsh
- 2kHz: forward presence — boost to push element forward

### Upper Mids (2-8kHz)
- Most sensitive range for human hearing
- 2-4kHz: vocal presence, guitar pick attack, snare crack
- 3-5kHz: kick click, snare crack
- 4-6kHz: sibilance/de-essing zone
- 6-8kHz: air and sparkle

### Air (8kHz+)
- 8-12kHz: cymbal shimmer, vocal air, synth brightness
- 12-16kHz: shelf boost here for open, airy sound
- Boost air AFTER compression — boosting before acts as de-essing

## COMPRESSION GUIDE

### Attack & Release
- Fast attack (0.1-5ms): tames transients, reduces punch — tightening
- Slow attack (10-50ms): lets transients through, adds punch — kick, snare
- Fast release (50-200ms): density, can pump — parallel drums
- Slow release (200ms-1s): smooth, natural — vocals, full mix

### Ratio Guide
- 2:1 — transparent, mix bus, gentle control
- 4:1 — moderate, audible but musical — vocals, bass
- 8:1 — firm, aggressive parallel drums
- 10:1+ — limiting, peak control

### Compressor Types
- 1176 (FET): fast and aggressive — vocal excitement, parallel drums, kick snap
- LA-2A (Optical): smooth, program-dependent — vocals, bass, acoustics
- VCA (SSL/Glue): tight and punchy — mix bus, drum bus, group bus
- Variable-Mu: warm and vintage — mix bus glue

### New York Parallel Compression (Scheps Method)
1. Create return channel from track or drum bus
2. Compress HEAVILY on return (30:1, very low threshold)
3. Blend return at -20 to -30dB below dry signal
4. Result: dry transients + compressed sustain = punchy and dense

## PSYCHOACOUSTICS

### Loudness Illusions
- Louder ALWAYS sounds better in A/B — level-match before comparing
- Dynamic range creates PERCEIVED loudness — quiet verse makes chorus feel louder
- Train yourself: turn down the louder version and ask if it still sounds better

### Fletcher-Munson Curves
- Human hearing most sensitive at 2-5kHz
- We hear LESS bass at low volumes — mix at moderate levels (75-80dB SPL)
- Check at low volume: if bass disappears, it was only "loudness effect"
- CLA: mix quietly, check at loud for excitement

### Masking
- Frequency masking: two sounds at same frequency = one disappears
- Fix: EQ carving, sidechaining, panning, arrangement

### The 3D Soundstage
- Left-Right: panning
- Front-Back: reverb amount (more = further back) + high frequency content
- Up-Down: EQ (high freq = "higher" perceived position)
- Depth: roll off highs on background elements, add pre-delay to reverb sends

## MASTER TECHNIQUES FROM THE PROS

### The Ghenea Contrast Trick
- Apply HPF AND LPF on master bus automation
- Verse: filters ON (narrower frequency range)
- Chorus: filters OFF (full frequency range) — instant huge impact
- Chorus sounds HUGE without raising a fader

### CLA Lightning Speed EQ
- Solo instrument
- Sweep high shelf up until too bright → back off just before that point
- This takes 10-15 seconds total — don't overthink
- Repeat for low shelf and high-mid presence

### The Pultec Trick (Low End)
- Boost AND cut the same frequency on Pultec-style EQ
- Boost 60Hz AND cut 100Hz = tight, punchy low end
- In Ableton EQ Eight: boost at 60Hz (bell) + cut at 100-120Hz (shelf or bell)

### Kick/Bass Frequency Separation
- Find kick fundamental (50-80Hz) — boost here on kick, CUT on bass
- Find bass fundamental — boost here on bass, CUT on kick
- Both occupy own frequency space — both audible without masking
- Also try sidechaining: bass ducks slightly when kick hits

### Mono Compatibility Check
1. Utility device → Width to 0 (mono sum)
2. Check: does bass disappear? (phase cancellation = fix)
3. Do elements disappear completely? (phase problem = fix panning/phase)
4. Club systems often run mono — critical check for dance music

### Reference Track Workflow
1. Import commercial reference into Ableton
2. Level-match: reduce reference gain 6-10dB to match your mix
3. Spectrum analyzer: compare frequency balance
4. A/B rapidly in first 3 seconds — ears reset quickly
5. Note: more/less bass? brighter/darker? wider/narrower?

## COMMON GENRE SIGNATURES

### Electronic / House
- Kick: 60Hz punch, 3-5kHz click, 80-90dB in mix
- Bass: sidechained to kick, 80-120Hz body, sub below 60Hz
- Pads: wide stereo, high-passed 200-400Hz, reverb tail
- Hi-hats: bright, 8kHz+, velocity varied

### Hip-Hop / Trap
- 808: tuned to key, long decay (2-4s), mono low end
- Snare/clap: heavy reverb, 200Hz body, 5kHz snap
- Hi-hats: rolls at 1/16, 1/32, velocity automation essential
- Vocal: very forward, no excessive reverb, dry and present

### Pop
- Vocal: center and forward, everything else serves it
- Production: contrast verse/chorus, build and release
- Low end: clean, punchy, sub audible but controlled
- Wide: pads and guitars spread, kick/bass/lead mono

### Rock
- Guitar: LCR panning for doubles, mid-range weight
- Drums: punch and attack, parallel compression
- Vocal: present but sits with the band, not above it
- Low end: tight, not as prominent as in electronic music
