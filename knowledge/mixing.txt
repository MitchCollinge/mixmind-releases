# MIXING PHILOSOPHY & PREFERENCES

## General Approach
- Always gain stage before reaching for EQ or compression
- Mix at low volumes (around 75dB SPL) and check at loud volumes for excitement
- Check in mono regularly — if it sounds good in mono, it sounds great in stereo
- Leave 3–6dB of headroom on the master bus before mastering
- Reference against commercial tracks in the same genre frequently

## Drum Treatment
- Kick and bass should occupy different frequency ranges — sidechain or duck if needed
- Parallel compress drums on a return track for punch without killing dynamics
- Hi-hats and cymbals: high-pass aggressively below 8kHz, keep airy not harsh
- Snare needs body (200Hz) and snap (4–8kHz) — cut the boxiness at 400–600Hz
- Drum bus glue compression: Glue Compressor, ratio 2:1, slow attack, auto release, 1–3dB GR

## Bass Treatment
- Sub bass should be mono below 80–100Hz — use Utility to check
- Bass and kick: carve space using dynamic EQ or sidechain compression
- Keep bass volume consistent — automation if needed for variation in arrangement
- Low-end check: always A/B on headphones and speakers

## Stereo Field Rules
- Kick, bass, sub, lead vocal, lead synth → center always
- Pads, guitars, atmospheres → wide (±0.3–0.6)
- Nothing hard panned unless intentional (guitar doubles at ±0.8–1.0)
- Master bus: check width with Utility set to mono — lose anything important?

## Automation
- Volume rides on vocals are essential — automate every phrase
- Filter automation on pads/synths adds movement without changing the mix
- Reverb sends can be automated for builds — pull back on verses, push on choruses

## Return/Send Tracks
- Reverb return: high-pass at 300–400Hz to keep it clean, not muddy
- Delay return: sync to tempo, use ping-pong for width, filter highs
- Saturation/harmonic exciter on a parallel return for warmth
